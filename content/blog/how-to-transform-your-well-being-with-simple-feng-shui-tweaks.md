Feng Shui is more than just a trend or a decorative style—it’s a way of living that helps us feel better in our own homes. Many people think Feng Shui is only about how you arrange your furniture, where you put your windows, or the way your house is built. But its influence goes much deeper, often affecting our minds in ways we don’t even notice. Sometimes, we struggle to sleep, feel uneasy, or even get sick, and we don’t realize that the arrangement of our living space might be the cause.

Feng Shui is about harmony. It’s about organizing your environment so that you feel safe, comfortable, and at peace. This doesn’t just apply to homes; it can also make a difference in public spaces. In our world, where stress and anxiety are common, Feng Shui can help us feel happier and even healthier. Sometimes, just moving your bed or changing the way you sleep can make a huge difference in your energy and mood.

## The Invisible Impact of Space

Before I learned about Feng Shui, I didn’t pay much attention to how my surroundings affected me. But with experience, I noticed certain patterns. For example, sitting in a room with an open door behind me always made me feel uneasy. If I was in a room with big windows on both sides, I felt exposed and uncomfortable, wanting to close the curtains or move away from the windows. After reading a book on Feng Shui, I realized these were not coincidences. I was sitting in “negative zones” where the energy, or “Qi,” was not good for my well-being.

Feng Shui, to me, is a philosophy and a kind of common sense. It’s about feeling good in your home. For example, in the bathroom—a place for self-care and relaxation—sharp angles in furniture are best avoided. Rounded shapes are gentler and help you feel safe and calm. If you have a piece of furniture with a sharp corner pointing at your bed, it can feel aggressive. In the living room, an oval table is more pleasant for meals than a table with sharp corners, especially if the table is between two windows, which creates a draft of energy that can make you feel insecure.

## The Power of Position

Where you place your bed matters. If your bed is between the door and the window, you’re in the path of the Qi, which is not good. I used to sleep in any direction, but I always felt better with my head pointing north—something I later learned is recommended in Feng Shui. Sometimes, we follow these principles instinctively, without even realizing it.

The same goes for your desk. If there’s an open door behind you, you may not feel fully relaxed or focused, because part of your mind is always alert to what might come through that door. It’s a survival instinct. Rounded furniture and soft shapes help us feel less threatened, while beams on the ceiling or low, uneven ceilings can create a sense of pressure or discomfort.

## Mirrors, Light, and Entryways

Mirrors are another important element. Never place a mirror directly facing your bed—it’s unsettling to see your reflection as soon as you wake up. Also, don’t put a mirror facing the front door, as it reflects the energy back out of your home. Instead, place mirrors to the side of the entrance.

The entrance to your home sets the tone for the whole house. It should be clear, welcoming, and bright. If you walk straight into the living room from outside, try to create a small entry area with plants or a shelf to separate the outside world from your private space. This helps you and your guests feel more secure and at home.

## The Shape of Your Land and Home

The shape of your property matters too. A rectangular plot is better than a triangular one, and it’s best not to live at the very end of a street or in a house that feels isolated. Being surrounded by other homes or trees can help you feel protected. Avoid having your bedroom face a paved courtyard, a factory, a cemetery, or a highway. If you can’t avoid it, use a thick hedge or trees to block negative energy.

## The Flow of Energy in Everyday Life

Keep bathroom doors and toilet lids closed to prevent energy from escaping. In the kitchen, don’t place the stove in the middle of the room; it should be against a wall. If your kitchen is open to the living room, use plants or shelves to create a small separation and redirect the flow of energy.

When storing food, keep it in dark, cool places, and avoid having the kitchen door open at the same time as the front door, as this can let energy rush in and spoil your food.

## Small Adjustments, Big Differences

If you can’t change the structure of your home, you can still make small changes—move your bed, add plants, or create a virtual entryway. Even these little adjustments can make a big difference in how you feel.

Feng Shui is a vast and fascinating subject. Sometimes, we feel uneasy in a hotel room or a new house and think it’s just our imagination. But often, it’s the energy of the space. With a few simple changes, you can improve your well-being and create a home that truly supports you.

---

## Key Takeaways
- The way you arrange your home affects your mood, health, and sense of security.
- Avoid sharp angles, especially in bedrooms and bathrooms; choose rounded furniture.
- Don’t place beds or desks in the direct path between doors and windows.
- Keep entryways clear, bright, and welcoming; avoid mirrors facing the front door or bed.
- Use plants or shelves to create separations if your home is open-plan.
- Close bathroom doors and toilet lids to keep energy from escaping.
- Small changes can make a big difference in how you feel at home.

## Action Steps
- Check the position of your bed and desk—move them out of the path between doors and windows.
- Add plants or shelves to create gentle separations in open spaces.
- Remove or reposition mirrors that face your bed or front door.
- Make your entryway bright and clutter-free.

- Do you feel truly comfortable and safe in every room of your home?
- Are there places where you always feel uneasy or restless?
