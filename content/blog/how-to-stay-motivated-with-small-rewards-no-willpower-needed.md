Have you ever noticed how much easier it is to finish something when there’s a little treat waiting for you at the end? I want to share a simple but powerful principle that keeps me motivated every day: the reward trigger.

Let me give you a real-life example. Right now, I’m wrapping up a new feature for my SaaS software. It’s not the flashiest update—just some work on the DNS infrastructure for subdomains—but I’m almost done. And you know what’s pushing me to finish? The thought of a delicious brunch: a flat white with oat milk and a splash of healthy caramel syrup. That’s my little pleasure, my reward for getting through the task.

I haven’t eaten yet, and I’m hungry, so I’m racing to finish. This isn’t just about food, though. Sometimes, for bigger projects, I set bigger rewards. For example, I might promise myself a new iPhone—but only if I hit my goal of earning €3,000 a month from freelancing, on top of my regular job. If I don’t reach the goal, no iPhone. It’s that simple.

Setting rewards gives you a real incentive. But there’s another piece to this: deadlines. If you don’t set a clear deadline, you’ll keep pushing things off—“I’ll do it tomorrow, or maybe the day after…” and nothing gets done. But if you say, “I have to finish this by the day after tomorrow to get my reward,” suddenly, you’re focused. The deadline makes it real.

Some people use this trick for all sorts of things. Maybe you finish a training session and treat yourself to a cold beer. If you’re in a hot, humid place where beer is cheap, that’s a real pleasure. For me, living in Australia, it’s all about coffee culture. When I lived in Sydney, it was more about wine, like in France—sharing a glass with friends at a restaurant. Now, I work in places that make me feel good, surrounded by nature, with a view of the bay. That view reminds me that anything is possible if I really want it.

Everyone starts at a different level. Some people have nothing, others already have a community or a network. But even if you start from scratch, you can always find a way—especially if you set a deadline and a reward. Even for small tasks, I tell myself, “If I finish this before the coffee shop closes at 3pm, I get my treat.” That’s another trick: time boxing. I put my work into a clear time slot—like, I have to finish before my laptop battery dies. It’s a simple way to stay on track.

The environment matters, too. If you’re in a place where everyone is working hard, you’ll feel like working hard too. If you’re in San Francisco, raising funds is easier—not just because of the money, but because everyone around you is hustling. The right environment and the right rewards create the triggers you need to get things done.

So, next time you’re struggling to finish something, try this: set a clear deadline, promise yourself a reward, and work in a place that makes you feel good. You’ll be surprised how much more you can achieve.

- Always give yourself a deadline—otherwise, you’ll keep delaying.
- Choose an environment that makes you feel good and helps you focus.
- Use time boxing: work within a set time frame to boost your motivation.
- Remember, everyone starts somewhere. The important thing is to keep moving forward.
