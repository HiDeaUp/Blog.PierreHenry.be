Yesterday, I met up with a friend who looked completely drained. He had just started a new job, but he didn’t like it at all. The only thing keeping him there was the higher salary, which had allowed him to move into a beautiful new apartment—three times more expensive than his last one. Now, he felt trapped. He didn’t know what to do, stuck between a job he disliked and a lifestyle he felt forced to maintain.

I told him, maybe the problem isn’t the job itself, but how he sees it. What if he could work from home in that amazing apartment? Or maybe he could find ways to make better use of his time. Sometimes, we take on too much, or we get overwhelmed by the big picture. We see the mountain of tasks ahead and feel crushed before we even start.

Instead, what if you focus on just the next small step? Don’t think about everything you still have to do. Just look at the next piece of the puzzle. When you break your work down into small, manageable tasks, it becomes less intimidating. You don’t get discouraged as easily, and you might even start to enjoy your work a little more.

Too often, we see our job as a never-ending list of chores. We think, “After this, I have to do that, and in three months, there’s even more.” It’s no wonder we end up seeing work as torture, something to survive rather than something to enjoy. But if you stop worrying about what comes next, you can start to appreciate each small task for what it is.

Imagine waking up and thinking, “Today, I just have to do this one thing. That’s pretty nice.” Tomorrow, it’s another small thing. The day after, another. Each step is a little win. Over time, these small wins can push you toward the career you want.

For my friend, even if he stays in this company, he could move up to a senior position. He’s already well paid and living in a fantastic apartment. The only problem is, he’s rarely home to enjoy it because he’s always at the office. I suggested he ask to work from home a few days a week. In his company, that’s possible. He worried it would make communication with colleagues harder, but honestly, there are always ways to make it work. Sometimes, we use these worries as excuses.

He also told me he never takes a real lunch break. I suggested he start taking small, enjoyable breaks—what I call “delicious breaks.” For example, take a coffee break at 10 a.m. and go for a short walk. At lunch, take a full hour if you can. He said his colleagues only take half an hour, but I told him, “If no one complains, why not take the time you need?” Just because everyone else does something a certain way doesn’t mean you have to follow. Don’t be a sheep. Live your life your way.

If you feel like your job is eating up all your time and happiness, try to find ways to turn it into something you can enjoy, even just a little. Break your tasks into small pieces, take real breaks, and don’t be afraid to do things differently from everyone else.

## Key Takeaways
- Break big tasks into small, manageable steps
- Focus on the present task, not the whole mountain ahead
- Take real breaks, even if others don’t
- Don’t just follow the crowd—find what works for you
- Your mindset can change how you feel about your job

## Action Steps
- Tomorrow, pick one small task and focus only on that
- Schedule a real break for yourself—coffee, walk, or lunch
- Ask yourself if you’re following others’ routines or your own

## Reflection
*Are you making your work harder than it needs to be? What small change could make your day better?*
