Motivation is a word we hear everywhere, but have you ever stopped to ask yourself what truly moves you? There’s a big difference between internal and external motivation, and understanding this can change the way you approach your daily life.

Let’s break it down. Internal motivation is when you do something for yourself, because you love it, because it brings you joy or satisfaction. The reward is built into the action itself. Think of a hobby you’re passionate about, or a project you do just for the pleasure of it. That’s internal motivation at work.

External motivation, on the other hand, is when you do something for a reward that comes from outside—like working for a salary. You might not love the job itself, but you do it for the paycheck. The task isn’t the reward; the outcome is.

Why does this matter? Because we all have moments when we put things off, when we procrastinate. Often, it’s because the task feels too tedious, or we haven’t made it easy enough to start. There’s friction. For example, I used to record my videos indoors, and it just wasn’t enjoyable. Now, I film by the bay, under the sun, and I actually look forward to it. Even though there’s an external motivation—building my reputation and expertise—the internal motivation is there too, because I genuinely enjoy the process.

But if your motivation is only external, it’s worth asking: is there a way to avoid the task altogether? Could you automate it, or delegate it to someone else? Life is too short to spend it doing things you dislike, just to get by. You want to be the main character in your own life, not just a spectator.

Sometimes we inherit the idea that work has to be painful or dull. But that’s just old thinking. We’re lucky to live in a time when it’s easier than ever to choose what we want to do. All you need is a laptop, an internet connection, and the willingness to look around and appreciate where you are. When you enjoy what you’re doing, the quality of your work naturally improves.

Here’s something I do that helps me remember what I learn: I teach it to others. Every time I discover something useful, I write it down, and then I share it. Teaching is the best way to really understand and remember new ideas. It’s not just about keeping knowledge to myself; it’s about passing it on, so others can benefit too.

So, don’t wait for the perfect moment. The best time to take action is now. Enjoy where you are, enjoy what you do, and let both internal and external motivations work together for you. You’ll not only feel better, but you’ll also see better results.

## Key Takeaways
- Internal motivation comes from doing things you love for their own sake.
- External motivation is about rewards from outside, like money or recognition.
- If you only feel external motivation, see if you can automate or delegate the task.
- Enjoying your work leads to better results.
- Teaching what you learn helps you remember and understand it better.

## Action Steps
- Identify what truly motivates you about your daily tasks.
- Adjust your environment to make your work more enjoyable.
- Share what you learn with others to reinforce your own knowledge.
- Don’t wait—start today, even if it’s just a small step.

## Reflection
- What tasks do I do just for the reward, and which ones do I genuinely enjoy?
- How can I make my daily work more enjoyable?
- What’s one thing I can teach someone else today?
